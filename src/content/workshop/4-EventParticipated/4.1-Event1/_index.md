# Event 1: AWS First Cloud Journey Community Day

## Event information

| Info | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Date & Time | 09:00 – 12:00, June 13, 2026 |
| Location | Floor 26, Bitexco Financial Tower |
| Format | In person |
| Role | Attendee |
| Main topics | Career mindset, real-world DevOps, the FCAJ community journey, and a URL Shortener solution on AWS |

## 1. Why I attended

AWS First Cloud Journey Community Day is a community event for students and newcomers to cloud computing. I went to hear practitioners talk about how they think about the job, how they build skills before graduation, and what a real system on AWS actually looks like. I also wanted to observe the community up close to picture the path after this internship.

## 2. Main content

### 2.1. Career mindset and skills

The opening talk targeted tech students close to graduation. Instead of tools or specific technologies, the speaker focused on critical thinking, the habit of breaking a real problem down into pieces you can actually solve, and a framing for growing capability over time. One point that stuck with me was the description of how multinationals work: expectations around communication, documentation, and asking questions instead of waiting for tasks. It made me look again at my own study and work habits.

### 2.2. The reality of DevOps work

The second speaker walked through what a DevOps engineer actually does day to day. The recurring emphasis was on fundamentals: Linux, networking, CI/CD, and systems thinking. Tools change constantly, so if you only learn tools, every stack change means starting over. If you understand why a pipeline is designed a certain way, picking up any tool is fast. It gave me a clearer direction for the next phase: go back and shore up the basics rather than chase new tools.

### 2.3. The community journey

This part was a story from people further along in the First Cloud AI Journey program. They described going from being students, joining study groups, and eventually becoming AI engineers, young team leads, and AWS development partners. What I took away was less the success arc and more how they described the boring middle: labs that went nowhere, bad talks, groups that fell apart and reformed. Community was what kept them moving through those stretches.

### 2.4. Technical solution: URL Shortener on AWS

The final talk was a technical case study: building an auto-scaling URL shortener. The speaker presented a real architecture on AWS, starting from requirements (very read-heavy, low latency) and walking through each component and why it was chosen. What I liked was that it went beyond a diagram into trade-offs: when caching is worth it, when eventual consistency is acceptable, and when serverless beats containers for scaling. As someone still learning AWS, this was the most concrete example of the morning.

## 3. Takeaways

Technically, I saw the value of the fundamentals more clearly. Linux, networking, CI/CD, and systems thinking are less shiny than a new AWS service, but they set how far you can go.

Career-wise, I noted how the speakers described working culture at larger companies: proactive, clear communication, disciplined notes. Those are things I can practice during the internship rather than wait until I graduate.

On community, I saw that going alone burns out fast, and joining a study group with a steady rhythm is a sustainable way to keep progress over the long run.

## 4. Personal contribution

During the event I focused on notes for the DevOps and URL Shortener sections so I could cross-check them against what we cover in the study group. Afterwards I adjusted my study plan: spend time refreshing Linux and networking, build a small CI/CD pipeline end to end, and re-implement the URL shortener as a practice project to really feel the trade-offs the speaker described.

## 5. Conclusion

AWS First Cloud Journey Community Day was a solid morning: concrete technical content, honest career direction, and a real community angle that showed I am not walking this path alone. I left with a clearer to-do list for the rest of the internship.
